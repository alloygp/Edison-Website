2.5 What Edison Deliberately Doesn't Do

These anti-patterns are as differentiating as anything Edison does. Use them in content to signal values, not just services.

  • No Coupon Books — A dying dinosaur that management companies upsell for profit. Edison sends courtesy email reminders instead. Free. Modern. Respectful of homeowner money.
  • No Non-Competes — Edison earns loyalty through investment, not fear. Managers stay because they want to.
  • No Volume-Chasing — Portfolio growth is opt-in for managers. Edison will never sacrifice manager wellbeing — or community quality — for headcount.
  • No Manager Reports — Wasteful recaps of the past. Replaced by live Action Item Lists that give boards real-time visibility.
  • No Punitive Fine Culture — Edison actively talks boards out of fine-first enforcement policies. Correction is the goal, not revenue.
  • No Overloaded Managers — Industry standard is 15+ communities per manager. Edison's sweet spot is 8–10. If a manager can't do it well, Edison won't let them take it on.
  • Selective Client Partnerships — Edison is intentional about the communities it takes on. Boards that aren't aligned with a collaborative, reasonable management relationship aren't a good fit. This protects manager wellbeing and service quality. (Internal Context Only)

Section 4. Target Audience & Pain Points

4.1 Who They Are

Volunteer HOA, condo, and townhome board members in Central Florida. They have day jobs, families, and limited time. They took on board responsibility to make their community better — not to manage a management company.

4.2 What Keeps Them Up at Night

  • Their manager never calls back or emails back in time
  • They've been through 3 managers in 2 years and have to start over every time
  • They don't understand their financials and feel like they're flying blind
  • They're afraid of making a legal mistake as a volunteer
  • Changing management companies sounds like a nightmare — records, accounts, homeowner disruption
  • Their current company treats them like an account number, not a partner
  • Homeowners are frustrated and taking it out on the board

4.3 What They're Looking For

  • A manager who actually shows up, responds, and knows their community
  • Financial reporting they can understand and trust
  • Someone to take the burden off their plate without losing control
  • A partner who educates them, not just executes tasks
  • Confidence that switching won't be as painful as they fear

Section 5. Core Differentiators

Each differentiator includes the claim, the mechanism behind it, and the story or proof that makes it credible. Use all three layers when writing content — not just the label.

5.1 Industry-Low Manager Turnover

The Claim

Edison's managers stay. Boards stop starting over.

How It Works

Manageable portfolios of 8–10 communities (never the 15+ seen at competitors), opt-in growth model, unlimited PTO actively encouraged, no non-competes, remote flexibility, professional development investment, referral bonuses, and a buddy system for coverage.

Why It Matters to Boards

Manager turnover is the #1 reason boards leave management companies. Every time a manager leaves, institutional knowledge walks out the door and the board rebuilds the relationship from scratch. Edison's retention means the same manager who learned your community in year one is still there in year three.

Proof

Industry averages 30–40% annual manager turnover. Edison's is a fraction of that.

5.2 Boutique, Personalized Service — Not Volume-Driven

The Claim

Edison intentionally stays small enough to care about every community.

How It Works

Boutique by design. Relationship-driven rather than account-driven. Boards get consistent access, responsiveness, and a team that knows them by name — not by unit count.

Why It Matters to Boards

Large firms chase volume. Managers get overloaded. Communities become numbers. Edison's model is the deliberate opposite.

5.3 Dedicated Back-Office Structure

The Claim

Edison's managers aren't doing everything — they're doing the right things.

How It Works

Separate departments handle covenant enforcement, ARC review, accounting, and homeowner inquiries. The back office resolves 90% of routine homeowner requests without involving the manager. Managers focus on project management, board relationships, and proactive community leadership.

Why It Matters to Boards

When a manager isn't buried in homeowner calls and violation letters, they can actually be proactive — getting in front of issues before the board has to ask.

5.4 Leadership Credibility

The Claim

Edison is led by one of the most credentialed and recognized professionals in the industry.

How It Works

Tracy Durham holds the PCAM designation (one of the industry's highest), has 20+ years of experience, served as 2025 CAI Chapter President, sits on the CINC Advisory Board, and has been recognized nationally and locally with multiple awards.

Why It Matters to Boards

Boards are trusting Edison with their community's finances, legal compliance, and homeowner relationships. Leadership credibility signals that the firm knows what it's doing — and is invested in the industry's future.

5.5 Education-First Philosophy

The Claim

Informed boards make better decisions. Edison is committed to making that happen.

How It Works

Board certification tracking and support (required within 90 days in FL), continuing education hosting, Edison Education platform (In Development — targeting 2026), legislative advocacy through CEOMC, and regular fiscal training for managers so they speak financials confidently.

Why It Matters to Boards

Most management companies execute tasks. Edison teaches boards how to lead — which makes the whole community stronger.

5.6 Technology-Enabled, Not Tech-Overwhelmed

The Claim

Edison uses technology to make life easier — not to replace human service.

How It Works

CINC platform for financials, portals, and communication. WATTSON AI (Edison's branded assistant) for 24/7 resident self-service. Action Item Lists replace wasteful monthly reports with live, real-time project tracking. Plaud AI for automated meeting minutes. But when you call Edison — a person answers.

Why It Matters to Boards

Homeowners and boards get convenience and transparency without losing the human connection that actually builds trust.

5.7 Financial Stewardship

The Claim

Edison treats association finances like they're their own.

How It Works

Accounting through CINC Systems specialists, invoice documentation requirements, rapid vendor payment processing (not standard net-30 delays), reserve study support with boots-on-the-ground data, and banking partnership with One Florida Bank for reserve investment optimization.

Why It Matters to Boards

Financial mismanagement or opacity is one of the most common board complaints. Edison builds trust through accuracy, transparency, and proactive financial planning.

Dedicated enforcement department (separate from the manager), customized inspection frequency and policies per community, fair and consistent enforcement (inspectors see addresses, not names), ARC proactive follow-up, evening/special inspections available, monthly enforcement reports in board portal.

Central Florida's HOA management market is competitive and growing, with a mix of large regional firms (Sentry Management), multi-location operators (Empire Management Group), established legacy firms (Don Asher, since 1952), and mid-size local operators (Community Management Professionals, CMS Orlando). Empire Management Group has the strongest review footprint with multiple locations totaling 3,000+ Google reviews across Central Florida. Boards in this market have real options and are becoming more discerning — particularly around responsiveness, financial transparency, and transition support. Edison is seeing increased inbound interest from self-managed communities, signaling a growing segment of boards realizing they need professional support.

Section 10. Common Objections

These are the hesitations boards typically raise when evaluating Edison. Content writers should proactively address these on service pages and landing pages.

"Switching Management Companies Is Too Much Work."

Edison's response: The Edison Transition Experience is specifically designed to remove that burden. Extensive checklist, dedicated COO oversight, Meet Your Management event, record digitization, utility transfers — the board doesn't have to manage the transition. Edison does.

"You're a Smaller Company — Can You Handle Our Community?"

Edison's response: Boutique is the point. Smaller means your community isn't lost in a portfolio of hundreds. Edison's back-office departments — enforcement, ARC, accounting, collections — give communities enterprise-level support without the volume-driven tradeoffs.

"Our Current Management Company Is Fine."

Edison's response: "Fine" often means boards have lowered their expectations after years of mediocre service. The question isn't whether your current company is fine — it's whether your community is thriving.

"We've Changed Management Companies Before and It Didn't Help."

Edison's response: Most management company switches fail because the new company has the same structural problems as the old one — overloaded managers, high turnover, reactive service. Edison's model is structurally different.

"We Can Manage Ourselves."

Edison's response: Self-management works until it doesn't. The legal exposure, financial complexity, and homeowner conflicts that come with growth are exactly what volunteer boards weren't signing up for.

"Your Pricing Is Higher Than Competitors."

Edison's response: Edison's pricing reflects full-service depth — dedicated departments, proactive management, technology suite, and a manager who actually stays. The hidden cost of cheap management is constant turnover and reactive service. (Internal Context Only — use carefully, never be dismissive of price sensitivity)

Section 11. Local Market Context

Central Florida market dynamics that should inform content tone, targeting, and local relevance.

Market Overview

Central Florida — particularly the Orlando metro — is one of the fastest-growing HOA markets in the country. Rapid residential development, a large retiree population, and significant transplant communities create consistent demand for professional association management.

Market Dynamics

  • High volume of newer communities (5–15 years old) hitting major capital project milestones for the first time — roofs, roads, pools — creating demand for financially sophisticated management
  • Large 55+ and lifestyle community segment with high meeting frequency and active board cultures
  • Significant population of transplants from the Northeast and Midwest accustomed to more structured HOA management
  • Growing awareness of Florida-specific legislative requirements creating demand for management companies with strong compliance knowledge
  • Competitive market with a mix of large regionals (Sentry), multi-location operators (Empire), legacy firms (Don Asher), and boutique operators (Edison, CMS Orlando) — boards have real choices and are becoming more discerning
  • Increasing inbound from self-managed communities — boards hitting a complexity threshold and seeking professional support for the first time

What Central Florida Boards Care About Most

  • Manager responsiveness — a known pain point across the market, and a common negative theme in competitor reviews
  • Financial transparency — especially in communities that have been burned by mismanagement
  • Legislative compliance — Florida HOA law is active and boards are increasingly aware of their obligations
  • Technology access — portal adoption is high in this market, especially in younger communities
  • Transition support — the Orlando market has seen enough bad transitions that boards factor this into their decision
